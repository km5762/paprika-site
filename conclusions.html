Conclusions

 There are a few different conclusions that we can draw in relation to computing and society through the lens of the Paprika movie. The first has to do with the regulation of technology. Lots of new and innovative technologies such as AI in the real world and the DCMini in Paprika have the potential to do great things for society but also can cause great harm. AI has the potential to greatly help in places of automation, information processing, manufacturing, and much more for the future, but 37% of people said they are more concerned about the changes that come with AI than the potential benefits [1]. In the movie, a similar technological innovation is the DC Mini. This technology has the potential to be very helpful as seen through Detective Konakawa. By using the DC mini to process and grapple with his dreams, he gets led through psychotherapy and calls it a blessing that he wishes he knew about earlier([0] 24:00). But this tech isn’t without its harms. As seen in the movie at many points people were driven mentally insane through their use of the technology. Especially when connected with the almost virus-like dream parade. This caused people to lose hold of themselves and make rash decisions such as Dr Shima who in a crazed state, jumped out of a high building window. These potential downsides are things that need to be considered when creating and innovating new technology, and currently, there aren't enough people working on these issues. Over 100x the people are working in AI in comparison to working on its regulations to keep it safe [2]. Because of this, we have come to the conclusion that the government should regulate these private technology innovations more for the good of society. As an example, there are real-world instances where the government took a greater regulatory role and it led to positive regulation and increased security. When doing clinical studies the government has strict entrance requirements for safety and reliability [3] Another example is the technology Neuralink. Its brain implant technology is potentially very innovative but has been denied human trials due to its failure to meet FDA safety standards [4].

 Another conclusion that we drew had to deal with the accountability for the misuse of technology in a virtual world. We concluded that there should be increased accountability for our actions in digital space and online. The internet is a world completely separated from the real world and with it comes a way to hide our true selves. It is easier than ever to remain almost completely anonymous to the people we interact with online. This is also true for the virtual “dreamscape” that is created by the DC Mini. People are able to enter dreams almost like virtual reality, and people like Dr. Chiba can use this platform to hide behind a digital identity. Many of the main characters didn’t even know Paprika's true identity till the end of the movie. With this anonymity comes a great ease of misuse of tech and the corresponding digital spaces. This lack of connection to a physical person makes “crypto crimes” paying for crime via cryptocurrency, much easier to commit [5]. Alongside that, online harassment and cyberbullying are big problems because of their negative effects on people's well-being outside of these virtual spaces. Cyberbullying/Stalking or other tech misuse leads to lasting levels of high distress in over 30% of victims, and over 25% report having trouble sleeping after online abuse[6]. What makes it worse is that anonymity provides little to no consequences for these actions. This is why people want some sort of accountability for actions people take in the digital world. 81% of Americans say that laws need to be strengthened to hold cyberbullies accountable for their actions [6]. Other groups are clamoring for increased identification online in order to protect youth and other groups at risk of exploitation, antisocial, and fraudulent behavior. This is because, as we have seen in the movie as well, people's actions in these online spaces can have effects significantly outside their virtual homes. Whether that be the mental instability of the DC mini users, or the dream world literally merging with the real world and causing havoc and destruction along the streets, what we do online matters, and responsibility is needed to prevent these malicious outcomes.

 A third conclusion that we drew was that the use of technological devices provides deep insights into the user’s subconscious. In the movie, this connection is obvious: The DC mini quite literally allows the user to view their dreams, as well as the dreams of others. For many of the characters, this allows them a newfound form of expression that the real world lacks. Paprika, for example, navigates the vibrantly colored dreamworld as a whimsical fairy, whereas the real world is presented in drab grayscale ([0] 46:50). Even the way sequences are shot is different between the real world and the dreamworld: Notice that, before she enters the dreamworld, the cinematography is relatively static ([0] 46:34), whereas the dreamworld employs faster action cuts ([0] 50:50). Detective Konokawa gains insight into his repressed dreams of becoming a movie maker, and the trauma of his childhood friend dying. At the end of the movie we see this come full circle, as Konokawa speaks to his friend through a vision where he is told that “You just lived out our movie in real life” ([0] 1:24:20). For other characters, like Dr. Tokita, the DC mini brings to light underlying addictive tendencies. Throughout the film, Tokita is portrayed as someone addicted to their work ([0] 36:20) and food ([0] 19:48). The DC mini becomes the next object of his addiction, literally trapping him inside its dreamworld ([0] 45:30) Additionally, these same phenomena are present in real life. The smartphone is an example of a technology that exacerbated device addiction. Considering its ease of use and accessibility, more people than ever have one and are spending an hour more on average than on a computer [7]. Additionally, MySpace surveys have found that user’s tend to present idealized online personas, in a similar way that the characters in Paprika do [8]. Further studies also suggest that we model our behavior in real life after the traits our digital avatars exhibit. One such study, for instance, found that users with taller avatars are more likely to be more aggressive negotiators [9], further suggesting that our mental state is closely linked to our technological representations.
