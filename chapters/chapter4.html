Intellectual Property

 Although Paprika doesn't reference any form of government in it's narrative, and thus does not directly incorporate themes of intellectual property, there are still many questions we can ask about how ownership of intellectual property would work with regards to Paprika's primary plot device, the DC Mini. For instance, would dreams be considered intellectual property? This is a very difficult question. If dreams are to be considered intellectual property, they would likely fall under copyright law because they are original works of authorship and, by using the DC Mini, they become a tangible form of expression that others can experience. However the term 'authorship' is used very loosely here, as it is unclear who or what controls the events of a dream. If dreams using the DC Mini are lucid dreams, then the dreamer must be the author and their dream is considered a copyrighted work. On the other hand, if a dream is not dictated by the dreamer, then are they the author?; the dream may have come from their mind but they had no control over it's direction.

 We can further explore this question of ownership by analyzing a pair of contemporary cases involving the most recent disruptive technology: generative AI.

 Surprisingly, the DC Mini is not purely fiction, there is contemporary technology that has demonstrated some of the DC Mini's capabilities. A study published in March of 2023 explored a novel method for image reconstruction using diffusion models (a form of generative AI) and fMRI imaging of brain activity. For the study, the researchers used an existing dataset of fMRI data collected from test subjects while the subjects viewed various images. The researchers were then able to use an image diffusion model in combination with the fMRI data and textual image descriptions to recreate the images the subjects viewed with surprising fidelity. Many have speculated that this technology could eventually be used to reconstruct images from fMRI data gathered from subjects who are dreaming.

 Earlier that year in August of 2023, during the Thaler v. Perlmutter case, the District Court for the District of Columbia upheld a Copyright Office decision to decline Dr. Stephen Thaler's application for copyright registration. The copyright application that Thaler issued was for a work of visual art that was created by a generative AI system that Thaler created. This decision set a president that works of art created by generative AI are non-copyrightable.

 With these two examples in mind, we must ask the question: how much work does the DC Mini do to 'read' the person's dream? The film does not explain how the DC Mini works in much detail. However, if we presume the device uses some form of advanced algorithm, or generative AI–similar to the study mentioned above–then, based on the Thaler v. Perlmutter case, the dreamer's ownership of any record of their dream is not guarantied.

 How complex and generative does a tool have to be before an artist can no longer claim ownership of its output? How much of an active role does an artist have to play in the creation of a work for it to be considered theirs? Are you the author of your own dreams?
